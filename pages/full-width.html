Aristotle (384-322 BCE):

Aristotle, a student of Plato, made profound contributions to metaphysics in his work "Metaphysics."
He explored the nature of being, substance, and causality. Aristotle classified different kinds of existent things and introduced the concept of actuality and potentiality.
His metaphysical framework, emphasizing the study of being qua being, laid the groundwork for Western metaphysical thought for centuries.

John Locke (1632-1704):

A philosopher from the 17th century, played a significant role in promoting the idea of empiricism. According to Locke, all our knowledge comes from our experiences through our senses. He introduced the concept of "tabula rasa," which suggests that our minds start as blank slates, and we acquire knowledge by interacting with the world around us. His viewpoint highlights the importance of observing, experimenting, and using real-world evidence to develop our understanding.

Immanuel Kant (1724-1804):

Kant's moral philosophy, presented in works like the "Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals," proposed a deontological approach to ethics.

He argued for the existence of moral duties derived from a categorical imperative a universal principle that one should act according to maxims that could be willed as a universal law.
Kant's ethical framework emphasized the importance of moral duty and autonomy.

Arthur Schopenhauer (1788-1860):

Schopenhauer, in his work "The World as Will and Representation," extended his metaphysical ideas into aesthetics. He posited that art and beauty provide a temporary escape from the suffering inherent in the world. Schopenhauer's aesthetics focused on the sublime, the experience of profound beauty, and the role of art in transcending the mundane aspects of existence.
